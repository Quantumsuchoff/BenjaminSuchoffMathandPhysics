Force on a Circular Nail Tip
Problem

Nail tips exert tremendous pressures when they are hit by a hammer because they exert a large force over a small area. What force, in newtons, must be exerted on a nail with a circular tip of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$ to create a pressure of $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$?

Given

$d = 1.17\text{ mm} = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$

$P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$

Formula

Pressure equals force divided by area. For a circular tip, the area is $A = \pi r^2$ where $r = d/2$:

$$P = \frac{F}{A} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad F = P \cdot A = P \cdot \pi\!\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^{\!2}$$
Step-by-Step

Step 1 — Find the radius:

$$r = \frac{d}{2} = \frac{1.17 \times 10^{-3}}{2} = 5.85 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}$$

Step 2 — Calculate the area:

$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi(5.85 \times 10^{-4})^2 = 1.0752 \times 10^{-6}\text{ m}^2$$

Step 3 — Solve for force:

$$F = P \cdot A = (3.17 \times 10^9)(1.0752 \times 10^{-6}) = 3408\text{ N}$$
Answer
F = 3408 N
≈ 3.41 kN — the tiny area amplifies the force into enormous pressure
2
Hydrostatic Load on a Gas Tank
Problem

Consider a $0.505\text{ m}$-wide by $0.89\text{ m}$-long gas tank that can hold $49\text{ kg}$ of gasoline. Part (a): What pressure, in N/m², is exerted on the bottom of the gas tank by the weight of the gasoline when it is full? Part (b): What is the ratio of atmospheric pressure to that pressure?

Given

Width $w = 0.505\text{ m}$, Length $l = 0.89\text{ m}$, Mass $m = 49\text{ kg}$

$P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$

Formula
$$P = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{mg}{w \cdot l}$$
Step-by-Step

Part (a) — Pressure from the gasoline:

$$A = (0.505)(0.89) = 0.4495\text{ m}^2$$
$$P = \frac{mg}{A} = \frac{(49)(9.8)}{0.4495} = \frac{480.2}{0.4495} = 1068\text{ Pa}$$

Part (b) — Ratio of atmospheric to gasoline pressure:

$$\frac{P_0}{P} = \frac{101{,}325}{1068} = 94.9$$
Answers
P = 1068 Pa
Part (a)
P₀/P = 94.9
Part (b) — atmosphere is ~95× stronger

Mercury Manometer — Jar of Peanuts
Problem

A manometer filled with mercury measures the pressure inside a sealed jar of peanuts. The mercury level on the side open to the air is $9.3\text{ cm}$ lower than on the side connected to the jar. The density of mercury is $13.5\text{ g/cm}^3$. Part (a): Find the gauge pressure in the jar (Pa). Part (b): Find the absolute pressure in the jar (Pa).

Given

$h = 9.3\text{ cm} = 0.093\text{ m}$, $\rho_{Hg} = 13{,}500\text{ kg/m}^3$

Open side is lower → jar is below atmospheric pressure → gauge pressure is negative

Formula
$$P_g = -\rho g h \qquad P_{abs} = P_{atm} + P_g$$
Step-by-Step

Part (a) — Gauge pressure:

$$P_g = -(13{,}500)(9.8)(0.093) = -12{,}304\text{ Pa}$$

Part (b) — Absolute pressure:

$$P_{abs} = 101{,}325 + (-12{,}304) = 89{,}021\text{ Pa}$$
Answers
Pᵍ = −12,304 Pa
Part (a)
Pₐᵦₛ = 89,021 Pa
Part (b)
4
Breathing Through a Reed Underwater
Problem

In movies, heroes hide underwater and breathe through a hollow reed. You cannot inhale if your lungs are more than $60.0\text{ cm}$ below the surface. What is the maximum negative gauge pressure (in cm H₂O) you can create in your lungs on dry land, given that you can achieve $-3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$ when your lungs are $60.0\text{ cm}$ below the surface?

Given

Depth: $d = 60.0\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$; lung effort at depth: $-3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$

Key Concept
$$P_{required} = -(P_{water} + |P_{breathing}|)$$
Step-by-Step
$$P = -(60.0 + 3.15) = -63.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$$
Answer
P = −63.15 cm H₂O
Required — impossible, since lungs max out at only −3.15 cm H₂O

Fraction of Ice Submerged in Freshwater
Problem

Assume $\rho_{ice} = 910.9\text{ kg/m}^3$ and freshwater $\rho_w = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$. What fraction of an ice block is submerged when it floats?

Given

$\rho_{ice} = 910.9\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\quad \rho_{water} = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$

Formula — Archimedes' Principle
$$f = \frac{V_{sub}}{V_{total}} = \frac{\rho_{ice}}{\rho_w}$$
Step-by-Step
$$f = \frac{910.9}{1000} = 0.9109$$
Answer
f = 0.9109
91.09% submerged — only ~9% peeks above water

Half-Submerged Cube on a String
Problem

A cube with $L = 0.13\text{ m}$ and $\rho_c = 3.9 \times 10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$ is exactly half submerged in liquid of $\rho_l = 0.81 \times 10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$, on a string. Find: (c) weight $W$, (e) buoyant force $F_b$, (g) tension $T$. (h) If submerged more, what happens to $T$?

Given

$L = 0.13\text{ m}$, $\rho_c = 3900\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\rho_l = 810\text{ kg/m}^3$, $V_{sub} = L^3/2$

Formulas
$$W = \rho_c g L^3 \qquad F_b = \rho_l g \frac{L^3}{2} \qquad T = W - F_b$$
Step-by-Step
$$W = (3900)(9.8)(0.13)^3 = 83.97\text{ N}$$
$$F_b = (810)(9.8)\frac{(0.13)^3}{2} = 8.72\text{ N}$$
$$T = 83.97 - 8.72 = 75.25\text{ N}$$
Answers
W = 83.97 N
(c)
Fb = 8.72 N
(e)
T = 75.25 N
(g)

(h) Tension decreases if submerged more.

Blood flows through an artery of radius $r = 2.3\text{ mm}$ at speed $v = 42\text{ cm/s}$. (a) Find flow rate $Q$ (cm³/s). (b) Volume $V$ passing through in $30.0\text{ s}$.

$$Q = \pi r^2 v \qquad V = Q \cdot t$$

$$Q = \pi(0.23)^2(42) = 6.98\text{ cm}^3/\text{s}$$
$$V = (6.98)(30.0) = 209.4\text{ cm}^3$$

(a)
V = 209.4 cm³
(b)

Air Speed in a Heating Duct
Problem

Main intake duct diameter $0.305\text{ m}$. House interior $12\text{ m} \times 19.5\text{ m} \times 2.8\text{ m}$. What is the average air speed in the duct if it cycles the entire house volume every $15\text{ min}$?

Given

$d = 0.305\text{ m}$; house $12 \times 19.5 \times 2.8\text{ m}^3$; $t = 900\text{ s}$

Formula — Continuity
$$Q = \frac{V_{house}}{t} \qquad v = \frac{Q}{A_{duct}}$$
Step-by-Step
$$V_{house} = 655.2\text{ m}^3 \qquad Q = \frac{655.2}{900} = 0.728\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$$
$$A = \pi(0.1525)^2 = 0.07306\text{ m}^2 \qquad v = \frac{0.728}{0.07306} = 9.96\text{ m/s}$$
Answer
v = 9.96 m/s
≈ 36 km/h inside the duct
9
Pitot Tube — Airspeed from Stagnation Pressure
Problem

A pitot tube uses a mercury manometer with $h = 0.195\text{ m}$. Given $\rho_{Hg} = 13.6 \times 10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$ and $\rho_{air} = 1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$, calculate the airspeed.

Given

$h = 0.195\text{ m}$, $\rho_{Hg} = 13{,}600\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\rho_{air} = 1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$

Formula — Bernoulli
$$\Delta P = \rho_{Hg}\,g\,h \qquad v = \sqrt{\frac{2\,\Delta P}{\rho_{air}}}$$
Step-by-Step
$$\Delta P = (13{,}600)(9.8)(0.195) = 25{,}989.6\text{ Pa}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 25{,}989.6}{1.29}} = \sqrt{40{,}294} = 200.7\text{ m/s}$$
Answer
v = 200.7 m/s
≈ 723 km/h
10
Sump Pump — Bernoulli with Elevation & Diameter Change
Problem

Sump pump: $Q = 0.599\text{ L/s}$, $P_1 = 1.62 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$, pipe $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$. (a) Water rises $H = 2.09\text{ m}$, same diameter — find $P_2$. (b) Pipe widens to $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$, drops $h = 0.51\text{ m}$ — find $P_3$.

Given

$Q = 5.99 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$, $P_1 = 1.62 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$, $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$, $H = 2.09\text{ m}$, $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$, $h = 0.51\text{ m}$

Formula — Bernoulli
$$P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g z_1 = P_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g z_2$$
Step-by-Step
v₁ = Q/A₁ = 5.99×10⁻⁴ / π(0.01315)² = 1.103 m/s Part (a): same diameter → v₂ = v₁ → KE terms cancel P₂ = P₁ − ρgH = 162,000 − (1000)(9.8)(2.09) = 141,518 Pa Part (b): A₃ = π(0.03055)² = 2.932×10⁻³ m² v₃ = 5.99×10⁻⁴ / 2.932×10⁻³ = 0.2043 m/s z₃ = 2.09 − 0.51 = 1.58 m P₃ = 162,000 + 500(1.103² − 0.204²) − (1000)(9.8)(1.58) = 162,000 + 587.7 − 15,484 = 147,104 Pa
Answers
P₂ = 141,518 Pa
(a)
P₃ = 147,104 Pa
(b)
The Physics I method: find the formula, identify knowns and unknowns, substitute, compute.
For the mathematical foundation behind each step, try A Mathematician's Way.

Force on a Circular Nail Tip — Uniform Pressure on a Disk
Problem

Nail tips exert tremendous pressures when they are hit by a hammer because they exert a large force over a small area. What force, in newtons, must be exerted on a nail with a circular tip of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$ to create a pressure of $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

You see the word pressure and immediately think: pressure is a scalar field. More precisely, $P = dF/dA$ — the instantaneous rate at which normal force accumulates per unit area. The question gives you $P$ and asks for $F$. A mathematician's instinct: integrate $P$ over the domain $A$ to recover $F$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Pressure is spatially uniform across the tip — $P(x,y) = P_0 = \text{const}$. $P$ factors out of the integral completely.

A2: Domain is a closed disk $D$ of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$, so radius $r = d/2 = 5.85\times10^{-4}\text{ m}$.

A3: Force is purely normal (perpendicular) to the area — no shear.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$F = \iint_D P(x,y)\,dA = P_0 \cdot \iint_D dA = P_0 \cdot \text{Area}(D)$$
$$\text{Area}(D) = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^r \rho\,d\rho\,d\theta = \pi r^2$$
r = 1.17×10⁻³ / 2 = 5.85×10⁻⁴ m A = π(5.85×10⁻⁴)² = 1.0752×10⁻⁶ m² F = 3.17×10⁹ × 1.0752×10⁻⁶ = 3408 N
④ Answer
F = 3408 N
⑤ Intuition

Fix total force $F$ and shrink area $A \to 0$: pressure $F/A$ blows up. This is exactly why nails work — concentrate force onto a tiny area to achieve enormous local pressure with modest total force.

2
Hydrostatic Load on a Gas Tank — Weight as a Surface Force
Problem

Consider a $0.505\text{ m}$-wide by $0.89\text{ m}$-long gas tank that can hold $49\text{ kg}$ of gasoline. Part (a): What pressure in N/m² is exerted on the bottom? Part (b): Ratio of atmospheric to this pressure?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Gravity is a body force ($\rho g$ per unit volume). A mathematician asks: what is the force density acting on the bottom face? $P = F/A$, where $F$ comes from Newton: $F = mg$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Gasoline in static equilibrium — only gravity and the bottom reaction force.

A2: Bottom face: $0.505\text{ m} \times 0.89\text{ m}$. Pressure is uniform (lumped mass model).

③ Calculus Derivation
$$\sum F = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; F = mg = (49)(9.8) = 480.2\text{ N}$$
$$F = \iint_B P\,dA = P \cdot A \;\Rightarrow\; P = \frac{mg}{A}$$
P = 480.2 / 0.44945 = 1068.4 Pa Ratio: R = P_atm / P = 101325 / 1068.4 = 94.84
④ Answers
P = 1068 Pa
Part (a)
P₀/P = 94.84
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

The ratio ≈95 is striking: a full tank of gasoline exerts less than 1% of atmospheric pressure. Air pushes on surfaces far harder than gasoline does.

3
Differential Manometry — Integrating the Hydrostatic Gradient
Problem

A mercury manometer. Open side is $9.3\text{ cm}$ lower than jar side. $\rho_{Hg} = 13.5\text{ g/cm}^3$. Find gauge pressure and absolute pressure in the jar.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a boundary value problem for a first-order ODE. The pressure field in a static fluid satisfies $dP/dz = -\rho g$. You're given a height difference and asked for a pressure difference — literally: integrate the gradient.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Mercury is incompressible and static: $\rho = 13{,}500\text{ kg/m}^3$.

A2: Open side 9.3 cm lower than jar side → jar is below atmospheric pressure.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$\nabla P = \rho\vec{g} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{dP}{dz} = -\rho g$$
$$P_\text{atm} - P_\text{jar} = \rho g h \;\Rightarrow\; P_g = -\rho g h$$
P_g = −(13500)(9.8)(0.093) = −12,304 Pa P_abs = 101,325 − 12,304 = 89,021 Pa
④ Answers
Pᵍ = −12,304 Pa
Part (a)
Pₐᵦₛ = 89,021 Pa
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

$dP/dz = -\rho g$ means: going up in elevation, pressure decreases. Mercury higher on the jar side means the jar is pulling mercury up — it must be at below-atmospheric pressure.

4
Physiological Pressure Limits — Additive Pressure Scalars
Problem

Heroes breathe through a hollow reed. Lungs $60.0\text{ cm}$ below surface, achieving $-3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$. What max negative gauge pressure can you create on dry land?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a constraint satisfaction problem. Two pressure systems whose algebraic sum must equal zero for the person to inhale. Think: $P_\text{lungs} + P_\text{water} = 0$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$P_\text{ext}(z) = +z \;\text{cm H}_2\text{O (gauge)}, \quad z = 60\text{ cm}$$
$$P_\text{required} = -(60 + 3.15) = -63.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$$
④ Answer
P = −63.15 cm H₂O
⑤ Intuition

On dry land, lungs fight only atmospheric pressure. Underwater, the water column adds extra force crushing the chest. Think of the lung as a spring trying to expand against two opposing forces at once.

5
Buoyancy Fraction of Ice — Equilibrium as a Fixed-Point Equation
Problem

$\rho_\text{ice} = 910.9\text{ kg/m}^3$, freshwater $\rho_w = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$. What fraction of ice is submerged when floating?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Write the equilibrium condition as an equation in $f = V_\text{sub}/V_\text{total}$, solve symbolically. No arithmetic until the algebra is done.

③ Calculus Derivation

Archimedes follows from integrating pressure over the submerged surface (divergence theorem: $\oint P\hat{n}\,dA = \rho g V_\text{sub}$):

$$\rho_w V_\text{sub} g = \rho_i V_\text{total} g \;\Rightarrow\; f = \frac{\rho_i}{\rho_w} = \frac{910.9}{1000} = 0.9109$$
④ Answer
f = 0.9109
⑤ Intuition

$g$ and $V_\text{total}$ cancel completely — the fraction depends only on the density ratio. This is why the iceberg rule is universal regardless of size.

6
Static Equilibrium of a Suspended Cube — Three-Force Balance
Problem

Cube $L = 0.13\text{ m}$, $\rho_c = 3900$, $\rho_l = 810\text{ kg/m}^3$, half submerged on a string. Find $W$, $F_b$, $T$, and the effect of deeper submersion on $T$.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

$\sum F = 0$ is a linear equation in $\{W, F_b, T\}$. Parameterize symbolically in terms of $(\rho_c, \rho_l, L, g)$, reduce to scalar, solve.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$W = \iiint_\text{cube} \rho_c g\,dV = \rho_c g L^3$$
$$F_b = \rho_l g \frac{L^3}{2} \qquad T = W - F_b$$
W = (3900)(9.8)(0.13)³ = 83.97 N F_b = (810)(9.8)(0.002197/2) = 8.72 N T = 83.97 − 8.72 = 75.25 N Part (h): more submersion → V_sub↑ → F_b↑ → T↓
④ Answers
W = 83.97 N
Fb = 8.72 N
T = 75.25 N
⑤ Intuition

Tension is what's "left over" after buoyancy partially supports the cube. Deeper submersion → more displaced fluid → more buoyancy → string relaxes.

7
Arterial Blood Flow — Flux Integral over a Circular Cross-Section
Problem

Artery radius $r = 2.3\text{ mm}$, speed $v = 42\text{ cm/s}$. Find $Q$ and volume in $30.0\text{ s}$.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

$Q = \iint_A \vec{v} \cdot \hat{n}\,dA$. Volume $= \int Q\,dt$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$Q = \iint_D \vec{v}\cdot\hat{n}\,dA = v\cdot\pi r^2 \qquad V = \int_0^{30} Q\,dt = Q \cdot 30$$
Q = 42 × π(0.23)² = 6.980 cm³/s V = 6.980 × 30 = 209.4 cm³
④ Answers
Q = 6.98 cm³/s
V = 209.4 cm³
⑤ Intuition

In one second, all the blood in a cylinder of length $v$ and cross-section $A$ passes through. Volume = how many such cylinders pass in time $t$? Exactly $Q \cdot t$.

8
Air Duct Velocity — Continuity / Conservation of Volume
Problem

Duct diameter $0.305\text{ m}$, house $12 \times 19.5 \times 2.8\text{ m}^3$. Air cycles every $15\text{ min}$. Find air speed in duct.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Conservation law: total volume flux is conserved. $Q_\text{room} = Q_\text{duct}$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$\oint_S \vec{v}\cdot\hat{n}\,dA = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; Q_\text{in} = Q_\text{out}$$
Q = 655.2 / 900 = 0.7280 m³/s A = π(0.1525)² = 0.07306 m² v = 0.7280 / 0.07306 = 9.96 m/s
④ Answer
v = 9.96 m/s
⑤ Intuition

Incompressible → can't "store" mass inside. Narrow duct → same $Q$ → higher speed.

9
Pitot Tube Stagnation Pressure — Bernoulli as a First Integral
Problem

Pitot tube, $h = 0.195\text{ m}$, $\rho_{Hg} = 13.6\times10^3$, $\rho_{air} = 1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$. Find airspeed.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Bernoulli's equation is the first integral of Euler's equation along a streamline. Integrate the momentum ODE to get a conserved quantity — total mechanical energy per unit volume.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$\frac{d}{ds}\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + P + \rho gz\right] = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \tfrac{1}{2}\rho_{air} v^2 = \Delta P = \rho_{Hg}\,g\,h$$
ΔP = (13600)(9.8)(0.195) = 25,989.6 Pa v = √(2 × 25989.6 / 1.29) = 200.7 m/s
④ Answer
v = 200.7 m/s
⑤ Intuition

Slowing fluid to zero at stagnation converts ALL kinetic energy to pressure. Mercury is ~10,000× denser than air — tiny $\Delta P$ → measurable column height.

10
Sump Pump Energy Conservation — Full Bernoulli
Problem

$Q = 0.599\text{ L/s}$, $P_1 = 1.62\times10^5\text{ Pa}$, $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$. (a) rises $H = 2.09\text{ m}$, same diameter. (b) widens to $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$, drops $h = 0.51\text{ m}$.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Bernoulli with all three terms varying: pressure, velocity (continuity), and elevation. Set the conserved quantity at point 1 equal to points 2 and 3.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gz = \text{const}$$
v₁ = 1.103 m/s Part (a): v₂ = v₁ → KE cancels P₂ = 162,000 − (1000)(9.8)(2.09) = 141,518 Pa Part (b): v₃ = 0.2043 m/s, z₃ = 1.58 m P₃ = 162,000 + 500(1.103² − 0.204²) − (1000)(9.8)(1.58) = 147,103 Pa
④ Answers
P₂ = 141,518 Pa
P₃ = 147,103 Pa
⑤ Intuition

Wider pipe (Part b): fluid slows (continuity) → kinetic energy converts back to pressure, partially compensating the elevation loss. This is the Venturi effect in reverse.

All derivations reduce to two ideas:
$F = \displaystyle\iint P\,dA$  (pressure as surface integral)  &  $\dfrac{d}{ds}\!\left[P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gz\right] = 0$  (Bernoulli as first integral).
Everything else is calculus.

Pressure-Force Mapping on a Circular Nail Tip
Problem

A nail tip has a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$. A uniform pressure of $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$ is applied. Find the total normal force $F$ on the tip.

Given

$d = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$, $\quad r = 5.85 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}$, $\quad P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$

Formula
$$F = P \cdot A = P \cdot \pi r^2$$
Evaluation
$$A = \pi(5.85 \times 10^{-4})^2 = 1.07513 \times 10^{-6}\text{ m}^2$$
$$F = (3.17 \times 10^9)(1.07513 \times 10^{-6}) = 3408.2\text{ N}$$
Answer
F = 3408.2 N
Pressure is uniform → $F = P \cdot A$; the tiny area amplifies force into enormous pressure
2
Hydrostatic Load in a Rectangular Tank
Problem

Pressure-Force Mapping on a Circular Domain
Problem

A nail tip has a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$. A uniform pressure field $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$ is applied. Find the total normal force $F$.

① Domain & Field Definition

The system consists of a two-dimensional circular manifold $S \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ bounded by diameter $d = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$. Across this boundary, a constant scalar pressure field is applied. Because the pressure is uniform, the rigorous surface integral $F = \iint_S P\,dA$ simplifies significantly — $P$ factors out, leaving the force as the simple product of pressure magnitude and total area.

② Governing Equation
$$F = \iint_S P\,dA = P \iint_S dA = P \cdot A$$
③ Boundary Conditions

$S \subset \mathbb{R}^2$, $\; d = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$, $\; r = 5.85 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}$

$P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$ (uniform scalar field)

④ Evaluation
$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi(5.85 \times 10^{-4})^2 = 1.075132 \times 10^{-6}\text{ m}^2$$
$$F = (3.17 \times 10^9)(1.075132 \times 10^{-6}) = 3408.2\text{ N}$$
Result
F = 3408.2 N
The uniform pressure field maps to a resultant normal force vector of magnitude 3408.2 N

Energy Conservation in Pumped Systems
Problem

Sump pump: $Q = 5.99 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$, $P_1 = 162{,}000\text{ Pa}$, $d_1 = 0.0263\text{ m}$. (a) Same diameter, $z_2 = 2.09\text{ m}$ — find $P_2$. (b) $d_3 = 0.0612\text{ m}$, $z_3 = 1.58\text{ m}$ — find $P_3$.

① Domain & Field Definition

The final problem investigates a dynamic pipeline manifold where the spatial coordinate $z$ and cross-sectional area $A$ vary along the streamline. To evaluate the changing scalar pressure field, we simultaneously satisfy two governing constraints: the Continuity equation ($v = Q/A$) to map velocity fields at varying cross-sections, and Bernoulli's equation ($P/\rho + \tfrac{1}{2}v^2 + gz = \mathcal{C}$) to track total mechanical energy. Foundational boundary conditions: $P_1 = 162{,}000\text{ Pa}$, $Q = 5.99 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$, and primary elevation shift $z_2 = 2.09\text{ m}$.

② Governing Equation
$$P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g z_1 = P_n + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_n^2 + \rho g z_n \qquad v = \frac{Q}{A}$$
③ Boundary Conditions

$Q = 5.99 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$; $\; d_1 = 0.0263\text{ m}$; $\; d_3 = 0.0612\text{ m}$; $\; P_1 = 162{,}000\text{ Pa}$; $\; \rho = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$; $\; z_1 = 0$, $z_2 = 2.09\text{ m}$, $z_3 = 1.58\text{ m}$

④ Evaluation
$$v_1 = \frac{Q}{\pi(0.01315)^2} = 1.103\text{ m/s} \qquad v_3 = \frac{Q}{\pi(0.0306)^2} = 0.204\text{ m/s}$$

Part (a): Same diameter → $v_2 = v_1$ → kinetic terms cancel:

$$P_2 = P_1 - \rho g z_2 = 162{,}000 - (1000)(9.8)(2.09) = 141{,}518\text{ Pa}$$

Part (b):

$$P_3 = P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_1^2 - v_3^2) - \rho g z_3$$
$$= 162{,}000 + 500(1.103^2 - 0.204^2) - (1000)(9.8)(1.58) = 147{,}103\text{ Pa}$$
Results
P₂ = 141,518 Pa
(a)
P₃ = 147,103 Pa
(b)
Pure Derivation method: domain → governing equation → boundary conditions → evaluate.
Every solution is traceable to the fundamental definitions of pressure, flux, and mechanical energy.

FLUID MECHANICS — GRAND UNIFIED FRAMEWORK V6.0

The Standard Operating Procedure

Three governing protocols. One procedural attack plan. Follow this SOP and the physics of any fluid system — static or dynamic, simple or complex — reduces inevitably to algebra. No memorization required. Only logic.

I.
The Nature of Fluidic Systems
Introduction & Philosophy
Core Principle

At its core, fluid dynamics is the study of continuous media — substances such as liquids and gases that deform continuously under applied shear stress. Rather than tracking discrete, isolated point masses, we analyze fluid behavior through continuous fields spread across a defined spatial region.

Purpose of This SOP

This Standard Operating Procedure serves as a universal roadmap for tackling fluid flow manifolds. To avoid the rote memorization of complex calculus derivations and isolated formulas, this guide constructs the governing equations directly from fundamental conservation laws — mass, momentum, and energy.

It provides a rigorous, step-by-step procedure to reduce generalized three-dimensional integrals into the practical algebraic tools needed to conquer any fluidic system.

Φ₁
Phase 1: Domain Archetyping
Before a single digit is written, the manifold must be bound.
Step 1 — Map the Manifold

Define the boundaries of $\Omega$. Identify all Inlets ($S_{in}$), Outlets ($S_{out}$), and Walls ($S_w$). Every fluidic problem lives inside a domain — your first job is to draw it.

Step 2 — Identify the State

Observe the Velocity Field $\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{r})$. This single observation routes you to the correct protocol:

$$\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \;\longrightarrow\; \textbf{Static Instance} \quad\text{(Go to Phase 2, Instance C)}$$
$$\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0} \;\longrightarrow\; \textbf{Dynamic Instance} \quad\text{(Proceed to Phase 2, Instances A \& B)}$$
Φ₂
Phase 2: Protocol Invocation
Force the physics to reduce into algebra through logical constraints.
Instance A
Instance A — The Mass-Flux Lock (Continuity)

Goal: Relate cross-sectional area to flow speed.

The Command:

$$\oiint_{\partial\Omega} \rho\,\mathbf{v} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \iiint_{\Omega} \rho \, dV$$

The Constraint: Assume Steady ($\partial/\partial t = 0$) and Incompressible ($\rho = \text{const}$).

The Result: $\displaystyle\iint_{S_1} \mathbf{v} \cdot d\mathbf{A} + \iint_{S_2} \mathbf{v} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$

Convergence
$$\boxed{A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2}$$

Use this to find an unknown $v$ or $A$.

Instance B
Instance B — Streamline Energy Conservation (Bernoulli)

Goal: Solve for Pressure or Velocity at specific points.

The Command:

$$\int \nabla P \cdot d\mathbf{r} + \int \rho\,\mathbf{v} \cdot d\mathbf{v} + \int \rho\,\mathbf{g} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$$

The Constraint: Assume Inviscid (zero friction) and Steady flow along a streamline.

The Result: $P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gz = \text{Constant}$

Convergence
$$\boxed{P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g z_1 = P_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g z_2}$$
Instance C
Instance C — The Gravitational Gradient (Statics)

Goal: Determine pressure at depth in a static fluid.

The Command:

$$\nabla P = \rho\,\mathbf{g}$$

The Constraint: Set $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$. Gravity acts only in the $-\hat{k}$ direction, giving $dP/dz = -\rho g$.

Convergence
$$\boxed{P = P_0 + \rho g h}$$
Φ₃
Phase 3: Symbolic Synthesis
Combine instances to collapse the system.
Step 1 — The Link

If you have two unknowns (e.g., $P_2$ and $v_2$), use Instance A to find $v_2$, then plug it into Instance B to solve for $P_2$. The protocols chain together.

Step 2 — Boundary Injection

Replace all symbolic parameters with known boundary conditions: $P_{atm} = 101{,}325\text{ Pa}$, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$, densities, geometry.

Step 3 — Final Execution

Isolate the target variable. The math is now trivial arithmetic. The physics was fully resolved in Phases 1 and 2.

Force on a Circular Nail Tip
Problem

Nail tips exert tremendous pressures when they are hit by a hammer because they exert a large force over a small area. What force, in newtons, must be exerted on a nail with a circular tip of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$ to create a pressure of $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$?

Given

$d = 1.17\text{ mm} = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$

$P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$

Formula

Pressure equals force divided by area. For a circular tip, the area is $A = \pi r^2$ where $r = d/2$:

$$P = \frac{F}{A} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad F = P \cdot A = P \cdot \pi\!\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^{\!2}$$
Step-by-Step

Step 1 — Find the radius:

$$r = \frac{d}{2} = \frac{1.17 \times 10^{-3}}{2} = 5.85 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}$$

Step 2 — Calculate the area:

$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi(5.85 \times 10^{-4})^2 = 1.0752 \times 10^{-6}\text{ m}^2$$

Step 3 — Solve for force:

$$F = P \cdot A = (3.17 \times 10^9)(1.0752 \times 10^{-6}) = 3408\text{ N}$$
Answer
F = 3408 N
≈ 3.41 kN — the tiny area amplifies the force into enormous pressure

Hydrostatic Load on a Gas Tank
Problem

Consider a $0.505\text{ m}$-wide by $0.89\text{ m}$-long gas tank that can hold $49\text{ kg}$ of gasoline. Part (a): What pressure, in N/m², is exerted on the bottom of the gas tank by the weight of the gasoline when it is full? Part (b): What is the ratio of atmospheric pressure to that pressure?

Given

Width $w = 0.505\text{ m}$, Length $l = 0.89\text{ m}$, Mass $m = 49\text{ kg}$

$P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$

Formula
$$P = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{mg}{w \cdot l}$$
Step-by-Step

Part (a) — Pressure from the gasoline:

$$A = (0.505)(0.89) = 0.4495\text{ m}^2$$
$$P = \frac{mg}{A} = \frac{(49)(9.8)}{0.4495} = \frac{480.2}{0.4495} = 1068\text{ Pa}$$

Part (b) — Ratio of atmospheric to gasoline pressure:

$$\frac{P_0}{P} = \frac{101{,}325}{1068} = 94.9$$
Answers
P = 1068 Pa
Part (a)
P₀/P = 94.9
Part (b) — atmosphere is ~95× stronger

Pitot Tube — Airspeed from Stagnation Pressure
Problem

A pitot tube uses a mercury manometer with $h = 0.195\text{ m}$. Given $\rho_{Hg} = 13.6 \times 10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$ and $\rho_{air} = 1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$, calculate the airspeed.

Given

$h = 0.195\text{ m}$, $\rho_{Hg} = 13{,}600\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\rho_{air} = 1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$

Formula — Bernoulli
$$\Delta P = \rho_{Hg}\,g\,h \qquad v = \sqrt{\frac{2\,\Delta P}{\rho_{air}}}$$
Step-by-Step
$$\Delta P = (13{,}600)(9.8)(0.195) = 25{,}989.6\text{ Pa}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 25{,}989.6}{1.29}} = \sqrt{40{,}294} = 200.7\text{ m/s}$$
Answer
v = 200.7 m/s
≈ 723 km/h

Force on a Circular Nail Tip — Uniform Pressure on a Disk
Problem

Nail tips exert tremendous pressures when they are hit by a hammer because they exert a large force over a small area. What force must be exerted on a nail with a circular tip of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$ to create a pressure of $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

You see the word pressure and immediately think: pressure is a scalar field. More precisely, $P = dF/dA$ — the instantaneous rate at which normal force accumulates per unit area. A mathematician's instinct: integrate $P$ over the domain $A$ to recover $F$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Pressure is spatially uniform — $P(x,y) = P_0 = \text{const}$. A2: Domain is a closed disk $D$ of diameter $d$. A3: Force is purely normal — no shear.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$F = \iint_D P(x,y)\,dA = P_0 \cdot \iint_D dA = P_0 \cdot \pi r^2$$
r = 5.85×10⁻⁴ m, A = π(5.85×10⁻⁴)² = 1.0752×10⁻⁶ m² F = 3.17×10⁹ × 1.0752×10⁻⁶ = 3408 N
④ Answer
F = 3408 N
⑤ Intuition

Fix total force $F$ and shrink area $A \to 0$: pressure $F/A$ blows up. This is exactly why nails work.

Problem

A $0.505\text{ m} \times 0.89\text{ m}$ gas tank holds $49\text{ kg}$ of gasoline. (a) Pressure on the bottom? (b) Ratio of atmospheric to this pressure?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Gravity is a body force ($\rho g$ per unit volume). $P = F/A$, where $F$ comes from Newton: $F = mg$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$F = \iint_B P\,dA = P \cdot A \;\Rightarrow\; P = \frac{mg}{A}$$
P = 480.2 / 0.44945 = 1068.4 Pa | Ratio = 101325 / 1068.4 = 94.84
④ Answers
P = 1068 Pa
Part (a)
P₀/P = 94.84
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

The ratio ≈95 is striking: a full tank of gasoline exerts less than 1% of atmospheric pressure.

Differential Manometry — Integrating the Hydrostatic Gradient
Problem

Mercury manometer, open side $9.3\text{ cm}$ lower than jar side. $\rho_{Hg} = 13.5\text{ g/cm}^3$. Find gauge and absolute pressure in jar.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a boundary value problem for a first-order ODE. The pressure field in a static fluid satisfies $dP/dz = -\rho g$. Integrate the gradient.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$\nabla P = \rho\vec{g} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{dP}{dz} = -\rho g \;\Rightarrow\; P_g = -\rho g h$$
P_g = −(13500)(9.8)(0.093) = −12,304 Pa P_abs = 101,325 − 12,304 = 89,021 Pa
④ Answers
Pᵍ = −12,304 Pa
Pₐᵦₛ = 89,021 Pa
⑤ Intuition

$dP/dz = -\rho g$ means: going up, pressure decreases. Mercury higher on jar side → jar is below atmospheric.

Problem

$\rho_\text{ice} = 910.9$, $\rho_w = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$. What fraction submerged when floating?

③ Calculus Derivation

Archimedes follows from integrating pressure over the submerged surface (divergence theorem):

$$\rho_w V_\text{sub} g = \rho_i V_\text{total} g \;\Rightarrow\; f = \frac{\rho_i}{\rho_w} = 0.9109$$

f = 0.9109

$g$ and $V_\text{total}$ cancel — the fraction depends only on the density ratio. Universal regardless of size.

Sump Pump Energy Conservation — Full Bernoulli
Problem

$Q = 0.599\text{ L/s}$, $P_1 = 1.62\times10^5\text{ Pa}$, $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$. (a) rises $H = 2.09\text{ m}$, same $d$. (b) widens to $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$, drops $h = 0.51\text{ m}$.

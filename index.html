1
Force on a Circular Nail Tip
— Uniform Pressure on a Disk
① How a Mathematician Reads This

You see the word pressure and immediately think: pressure is a scalar field. More precisely, P = dF/dA — the instantaneous rate at which normal force accumulates per unit area. The question gives you P and asks for F. A mathematician's instinct: integrate P over the domain A to recover F.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Pressure is spatially uniform across the tip — P(x,y) = P₀ = const. P factors out of the integral completely.

A2: Domain is a closed disk D of diameter d = 1.17 mm, so radius r = d/2 = 5.85×10⁻⁴ m.

A3: Force is purely normal (perpendicular) to the area — no shear.

③ Calculus Derivation

Pressure as a surface integral, then factor out the constant:

F = ∬_D P(x,y) dA = P₀ · ∬_D dA = P₀ · Area(D)

Area of a disk in polar coordinates:

Area(D) = ∫₀²π ∫₀ʳ ρ dρ dθ = ∫₀²π [ρ²/2]₀ʳ dθ = πr²

Plug in:

r = 1.17×10⁻³/2 = 5.85×10⁻⁴ m A = π(5.85×10⁻⁴)² = 1.0752×10⁻⁶ m² F = 3.17×10⁹ × 1.0752×10⁻⁶ = 3408 N
④ Answer
F = 3408 N
≈ 3.41 kN — equivalent to supporting ~347 kg
⑤ Intuition

Fix total force F and shrink area A → 0: pressure F/A blows up. This is exactly why nails work — concentrate force onto a tiny area to achieve enormous local pressure with modest total force. The integral says "add up all the little dF = P·dA contributions across the whole surface."

2
Hydrostatic Load on a Gas Tank
— Weight as a Surface Force
① How a Mathematician Reads This

You see "mass m, area A, find pressure." Gravity is a body force (ρg per unit volume). A mathematician asks: what is the force density acting on the bottom face? P = F/A, where F comes from Newton: F = mg.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Gasoline in static equilibrium — only gravity and the bottom reaction force.

A2: Bottom face is flat rectangular: 0.505 m × 0.89 m.

A3: Pressure from gas is uniform across the bottom (lumped mass model).

③ Calculus Derivation
∑F = 0 → F_reaction = mg = 49 × 9.8 = 480.2 N F = ∬_B P dA = P · (0.505)(0.89) → P = mg / A P = 480.2 / 0.44945 = 1068.4 Pa Ratio: R = P_atm / P = 101325 / 1068.4 = 94.84
④ Answers
P = 1068.4 Pa
Part (a)
P₀/P = 94.84
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

Weight distributes uniformly across the bottom. Double the base area (same mass) → pressure halves. The ratio ≈95 is striking: a full tank of gasoline exerts less than 1% of atmospheric pressure. Air pushes on surfaces far harder than gasoline does.

3
Differential Manometry
— Integrating the Hydrostatic Gradient
① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a boundary value problem for a first-order ODE. The pressure field in a static fluid satisfies a differential equation. You're given a height difference and asked for a pressure difference — literally: integrate the pressure gradient equation.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Mercury is incompressible and static: ρ = 13,500 kg/m³ constant.

A2: Only body force is gravity, in the −ẑ direction.

A3: Open side mercury is 9.3 cm lower than the jar side → jar is below atmospheric pressure.

③ Calculus Derivation

Euler's static equation → 1D ODE → integrate:

∇P = ρg⃗ → dP/dz = -ρg (z upward positive) Integrate from jar (z₁) to open side (z₂ = z₁ - h): ∫ dP = ∫ (-ρg) dz P_atm - P_jar = -ρg(-h) = ρgh P_g = P_jar - P_atm = -ρgh P_g = -(13500)(9.8)(0.093) = -12,303.9 Pa P_abs = 101325 - 12303.9 = 89,021.1 Pa
④ Answers
P_g = −12,304 Pa
Part (a)
P_abs = 89,021 Pa
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

dP/dz = −ρg means: going up in elevation, pressure decreases. Mercury higher on the jar side means the jar is pulling mercury up — it must be at below-atmospheric pressure. Integrating accumulates the weight of the fluid column between the two levels.

4
Physiological Pressure Limits
— Additive Pressure Scalars
① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a constraint satisfaction problem. Two pressure systems whose algebraic sum must equal zero for the person to inhale. Find the required value of one variable given the constraint. Think of it as: P_lungs + P_water = 0.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: For inhalation: lung pressure must equal surface air pressure → zero net ΔP across the lung boundary.

A2: Pressure is additive: P_chest = P_water_column + P_lung_gauge. Units: cm H₂O.

A3: Max negative lung pressure on dry land: P_lung,max = −3.15 cm H₂O.

③ Calculus Derivation
P_ext(z) = +z cm H₂O gauge (z = depth in cm) At z = 60 cm: P_ext = +60 cm H₂O Required lung pressure to inhale against this: P_required = -(P_water + |P_lung,max,dry|) = -(60 + 3.15) = -63.15 cm H₂O Achievable maximum: -3.15 cm H₂O → impossible.
④ Answer
P = −63.15 cm H₂O
Required (impossible — physiological limit is only −3.15 cm H₂O)
⑤ Intuition

Pressure is additive like scalars. On dry land, lungs fight only atmospheric pressure. Underwater, the water column adds extra force crushing the chest — the lung must overcome both simultaneously. Think of the lung as a spring trying to expand against two opposing forces at once.

5
Buoyancy Fraction of Ice
— Equilibrium as a Fixed-Point Equation
① How a Mathematician Reads This

You want a ratio: f = V_sub / V_total. Write the equilibrium condition as an equation in f, solve symbolically, then substitute numbers. No arithmetic until the algebra is done.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Static equilibrium: ∑F = 0 vertically.

A2: Archimedes: F_b = ρ_w · V_sub · g (weight of displaced fluid).

A3: ρ_ice = 910.9 kg/m³, ρ_water = 1000 kg/m³.

③ Calculus Derivation

Archimedes follows from integrating pressure over the submerged surface (divergence theorem: ∮P n̂ dA = ρgV_sub). Then:

F_b = W ρ_w · V_sub · g = ρ_i · V_total · g Cancel g, divide by (ρ_w · V_total): f = V_sub / V_total = ρ_i / ρ_w = 910.9 / 1000 = 0.9109
④ Answer
f = 0.9109
91.09% submerged — only ~9% visible above water
⑤ Intuition

g and V_total cancel completely — the fraction submerged depends only on the density ratio. Ice is 91% as dense as water, so it displaces 91% of its own volume. This is why the iceberg rule is universal regardless of size.

6
Static Equilibrium of a Suspended Cube
— Three-Force Balance
① How a Mathematician Reads This

∑F = 0 is a linear equation in {W, F_b, T}. Parameterize each force symbolically in terms of (ρ_c, ρ_l, L, g), reduce to one scalar equation, solve. Since all forces are collinear (vertical), it's a one-dimensional problem.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Static equilibrium → T + F_b = W (upward positive).

A2: Exactly half submerged: V_sub = L³/2.

A3: L = 0.13 m, ρ_c = 3900 kg/m³, ρ_l = 810 kg/m³, g = 9.8 m/s².

③ Calculus Derivation
W = ∭_cube ρ_c g dV = ρ_c g L³ = (3900)(9.8)(0.13)³ = 83.97 N F_b = ρ_l g (L³/2) = (810)(9.8)(0.002197/2) = 8.72 N ∑F = 0 → T = W - F_b = 83.97 - 8.72 = 75.25 N Part (h): more submersion → V_sub↑ → F_b↑ → T↓ (decreases)
④ Answers
W = 83.97 N
Part (c)
F_b = 8.72 N
Part (e)
T = 75.25 N
Part (g)
⑤ Intuition

Tension is what's "left over" after buoyancy partially supports the cube. If ρ_c = ρ_l, the cube floats freely and T = 0. Deeper submersion → more displaced fluid → more buoyancy → string relaxes.

7
Arterial Blood Flow
— Flux Integral over a Circular Cross-Section
① How a Mathematician Reads This

Flow rate Q is a flux integral: Q = ∬_A v⃗ · n̂ dA. What velocity field is assumed, and what is the domain A? Once Q is known, volume = ∫Q dt.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Plug flow — v is uniform across the cross-section.

A2: Cross-section is a disk of radius r = 0.23 cm.

A3: Q is constant over 30 s, so V = Q·t (no integration).

③ Calculus Derivation
Q = ∬_D v⃗ · n̂ dA = v · ∬_D dA = v · πr² = 42 × π(0.23)² = 42 × 0.16619 = 6.980 cm³/s V = ∫₀³⁰ Q dt = Q · 30 = 6.980 × 30 = 209.4 cm³
④ Answers
Q = 6.98 cm³/s
Part (a)
V = 209.4 cm³
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

Q = A·v: in one second, all the blood in a cylinder of length v and cross-section A passes through. Volume = how many such cylinders pass in time t? Exactly Q·t of them.

8
Air Duct Velocity
— Continuity / Conservation of Volume
① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a conservation law: total volume flux is conserved. You know the total volume that must move per unit time (room volume ÷ cycle time) and the pipe cross-section. Solve for the one unknown velocity: Q_room = Q_duct.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Air is incompressible (low speed): ∇·v⃗ = 0 → Q_in = Q_out.

A2: House volume 12 × 19.5 × 2.8 m³ exchanged every 15 min = 900 s.

A3: Duct cross-section: disk of diameter 0.305 m.

③ Calculus Derivation
∮_S v⃗ · n̂ dA = 0 → Q_in = Q_out = Q Q = V_room / t = (12 × 19.5 × 2.8) / 900 = 655.2 / 900 = 0.7280 m³/s A_duct = π(0.305/2)² = π(0.1525)² = 0.07306 m² v = Q / A = 0.7280 / 0.07306 = 9.96 m/s
④ Answer
v = 9.96 m/s
≈ 36 km/h inside the duct
⑤ Intuition

Incompressible → can't "store" mass inside. Room sets the demand Q; duct area sets the geometry; velocity adjusts to satisfy both. Narrow duct → same Q → higher speed.

9
Pitot Tube Stagnation Pressure
— Bernoulli as a First Integral
① How a Mathematician Reads This

Bernoulli's equation is the first integral of Euler's equation along a streamline. Integrate the momentum ODE to get a conserved quantity — total mechanical energy per unit volume. Apply as a two-point boundary condition to find velocity.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Steady, inviscid, incompressible flow; freestream speed v → stagnation v₂ = 0.

A2: No elevation change: z₁ = z₂, so ρgz terms cancel.

A3: Manometer reads ΔP = ρ_Hg · g · h, h = 0.195 m.

③ Calculus Derivation
Euler along streamline (arc length s): ρ v dv/ds = -dP/ds - ρg dz/ds Rearranging → first integral: d/ds [½ρv² + P + ρgz] = 0 → conserved! Apply point 1 → point 2 (v₂ = 0, same height): ½ρ_air v² + P₁ = P₂ ΔP = ½ρ_air v² ΔP = ρ_Hg g h = (13600)(9.8)(0.195) = 25,989.6 Pa v = √(2ΔP / ρ_air) = √(2 × 25989.6 / 1.29) = √40294 = 200.7 m/s
④ Answer
v = 200.7 m/s
≈ 723 km/h
⑤ Intuition

Slowing fluid to zero at stagnation converts ALL kinetic energy to pressure. Mercury is ~10,000× denser than air — even a tiny ΔP produces a measurable column height, amplifying small pressure signals into readable measurements.

10
Sump Pump Energy Conservation
— Full Bernoulli with Elevation + Velocity
① How a Mathematician Reads This

Bernoulli with all three terms varying simultaneously: pressure, velocity (pipe diameter changes → continuity forces velocity change), and elevation. Set the conserved quantity at point 1 (known) equal to the conserved quantity at points 2 and 3 (unknowns). Two equations, two unknowns.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Steady, inviscid, incompressible — Bernoulli holds.

A2: Point 1: P₁ = 1.62×10⁵ Pa, d = 2.63 cm, Q = 5.99×10⁻⁴ m³/s, z₁ = 0.

A3 (Part a): Same diameter to point 2 → v₂ = v₁, z₂ = H = 2.09 m.

A4 (Part b): D = 6.11 cm at point 3, z₃ = H − h = 2.09 − 0.51 = 1.58 m.

③ Calculus Derivation
Conserved: E = P + ½ρv² + ρgz = constant v₁ = Q/A₁ = 5.99×10⁻⁴ / π(0.01315)² = 1.103 m/s ── Part (a): same diameter → v₂ = v₁ → KE terms cancel ── P₂ = P₁ - ρg·H = 162000 - (1000)(9.8)(2.09) = 141,518 Pa ── Part (b): wider pipe, continuity ── A₃ = π(0.03055)² = 2.932×10⁻³ m² v₃ = Q/A₃ = 5.99×10⁻⁴ / 2.932×10⁻³ = 0.2043 m/s P₃ = P₁ + ½ρ(v₁² - v₃²) - ρg·z₃ = 162000 + 500(1.2166 - 0.04174) - (1000)(9.8)(1.58) = 162000 + 587.4 - 15484 = 147,103 Pa
④ Answers
P₂ = 141,518 Pa
Part (a)
P₃ = 147,103 Pa
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

Bernoulli = work-energy theorem integrated: pressure + kinetic + potential = const. Going uphill (Part a): potential rises → pressure drops. Wider pipe (Part b): fluid slows (continuity) → kinetic energy drops → converts back to pressure, partially compensating the elevation loss. This is the Venturi effect in reverse.

All derivations reduce to two ideas:
F = ∬P dA (pressure as surface integral)  &  d/ds[P + ½ρv² + ρgz] = 0 (Bernoulli as first integral of momentum).
Everything else is calculus.

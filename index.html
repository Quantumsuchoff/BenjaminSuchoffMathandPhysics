Force on a Circular Nail Tip
Problem

Nail tips exert tremendous pressures when they are hit by a hammer because they exert a large force over a small area. What force, in newtons, must be exerted on a nail with a circular tip of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$ to create a pressure of $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$?

Given

$d = 1.17\text{ mm} = 1.17 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$

$P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$

Formula

Pressure equals force divided by area. For a circular tip, the area is $A = \pi r^2$ where $r = d/2$:

$$P = \frac{F}{A} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad F = P \cdot A = P \cdot \pi\!\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^{\!2}$$
Step-by-Step

Step 1 — Find the radius:

$$r = \frac{d}{2} = \frac{1.17 \times 10^{-3}}{2} = 5.85 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}$$

Step 2 — Calculate the area:

$$A = \pi r^2 = \pi(5.85 \times 10^{-4})^2 = 1.0752 \times 10^{-6}\text{ m}^2$$

Step 3 — Solve for force:

$$F = P \cdot A = (3.17 \times 10^9)(1.0752 \times 10^{-6}) = 3408\text{ N}$$
Answer
F = 3408 N
≈ 3.41 kN — the tiny area amplifies the force into enormous pressure

Hydrostatic Load on a Gas Tank
Problem

Consider a $0.505\text{ m}$-wide by $0.89\text{ m}$-long gas tank that can hold $49\text{ kg}$ of gasoline. Part (a): What pressure, in N/m², is exerted on the bottom of the gas tank by the weight of the gasoline when it is full? Part (b): What is the ratio of atmospheric pressure to that pressure?

Given

Width $w = 0.505\text{ m}$, Length $l = 0.89\text{ m}$, Mass $m = 49\text{ kg}$

$P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$

Formula
$$P = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{mg}{w \cdot l}$$
Step-by-Step

Part (a) — Pressure from the gasoline:

$$A = (0.505)(0.89) = 0.4495\text{ m}^2$$
$$P = \frac{mg}{A} = \frac{(49)(9.8)}{0.4495} = \frac{480.2}{0.4495} = 1068\text{ Pa}$$

Part (b) — Ratio of atmospheric to gasoline pressure:

$$\frac{P_0}{P} = \frac{101{,}325}{1068} = 94.9$$
Answers
P = 1068 Pa
Part (a)
P₀/P = 94.9
Part (b) — atmosphere is ~95× stronger

Mercury Manometer — Jar of Peanuts
Problem

A manometer filled with mercury measures the pressure inside a sealed jar of peanuts. The mercury level on the side open to the air is $9.3\text{ cm}$ lower than on the side connected to the jar. The density of mercury is $13.5\text{ g/cm}^3$. Part (a): Find the gauge pressure in the jar (Pa). Part (b): Find the absolute pressure in the jar (Pa).

Given

$h = 9.3\text{ cm} = 0.093\text{ m}$ (height difference)

$\rho_{Hg} = 13.5\text{ g/cm}^3 = 13{,}500\text{ kg/m}^3$

Open side is lower → jar is below atmospheric pressure → gauge pressure is negative

Formula

The pressure difference due to a fluid column of height $h$:

$$P_g = -\rho g h \quad(\text{negative because jar} < P_{atm})$$
$$P_{abs} = P_{atm} + P_g$$
Step-by-Step

Part (a) — Gauge pressure:

$$P_g = -\rho g h = -(13{,}500)(9.8)(0.093) = -12{,}304\text{ Pa}$$

Part (b) — Absolute pressure:

$$P_{abs} = 101{,}325 + (-12{,}304) = 89{,}021\text{ Pa}$$
Answers
Pᵍ = −12,304 Pa
Part (a) — vacuum-sealed jar is below atmospheric
Pₐᵦₛ = 89,021 Pa
Part (b)

Breathing Through a Reed Underwater
Problem

In movies, heroes hide underwater and breathe through a hollow reed. In practice, you cannot inhale if your lungs are more than $60.0\text{ cm}$ below the surface due to water pressure on your chest. What is the maximum negative gauge pressure, in cm H₂O, you can create in your lungs on dry land, given that you can achieve only $-3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$ of pressure when your lungs are $60.0\text{ cm}$ below the surface?

Given

Depth of lungs: $d = 60.0\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$ of external water pressure

Pressure achievable by lungs at that depth: $-3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$ (relative to water outside)

Key Concept

To breathe at depth $d$, your lungs must overcome both the water column pressure and create the breathing pressure depression. Pressures add as scalars:

$$P_{required} = -(P_{water} + |P_{breathing}|)$$
Step-by-Step

The water at 60 cm depth pushes $+60\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$ inward on the chest. Your lungs must create enough negative pressure to overcome this AND still draw air in (the $-3.15$ cm H₂O breathing threshold):

$$P = -(60.0 + 3.15) = -63.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$$

Your lungs can only produce $-3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$ of effort — far short of the required $-63.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$. This is why breathing through a reed at 60 cm depth is physically impossible.

Answer
P = −63.15 cm H₂O
Required pressure — impossible, since lungs max out at only −3.15 cm H₂O

Fraction of Ice Submerged in Freshwater
Problem

The fraction of ice submerged in water depends only on the densities of ice and water. Assume the density of ice is $\rho_{ice} = 910.9\text{ kg/m}^3$. What fraction of an ice block is submerged when it floats in freshwater ($\rho_w = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$)?

Given

$\rho_{ice} = 910.9\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\quad \rho_{water} = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$

Formula — Archimedes' Principle

For a floating object, buoyant force equals weight:

$$\rho_w \cdot V_{sub} \cdot g = \rho_{ice} \cdot V_{total} \cdot g$$

Dividing both sides by $\rho_w \cdot V_{total} \cdot g$:

$$f = \frac{V_{sub}}{V_{total}} = \frac{\rho_{ice}}{\rho_w}$$
Step-by-Step
$$f = \frac{\rho_{ice}}{\rho_w} = \frac{910.9}{1000} = 0.9109$$
Answer
f = 0.9109
91.09% is submerged — only the top ~9% peeks above the water

Half-Submerged Cube on a String
Problem

A cube with edge length $L = 0.13\text{ m}$ and density $\rho_c = 3.9 \times 10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$ is exactly half submerged in a liquid of density $\rho_l = 0.81 \times 10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$, suspended by a taut string. Find: (c) the weight $W$, (e) the buoyant force $F_b$, (g) the tension $T$. (h) If the block is submerged more, what happens to $T$?

Given

$L = 0.13\text{ m}$, $\rho_c = 3900\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\rho_l = 810\text{ kg/m}^3$

Half submerged: $V_{sub} = L^3/2$; $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$

Formulas
$$W = \rho_c g L^3 \qquad F_b = \rho_l g \frac{L^3}{2} \qquad T = W - F_b$$
Step-by-Step

Weight (c):

$$W = \rho_c g L^3 = (3900)(9.8)(0.13)^3 = (3900)(9.8)(0.002197) = 83.97\text{ N}$$

Buoyant Force (e):

$$F_b = \rho_l g \frac{L^3}{2} = (810)(9.8)\frac{0.002197}{2} = (810)(9.8)(0.001099) = 8.72\text{ N}$$

Tension (g):

$$T = W - F_b = 83.97 - 8.72 = 75.25\text{ N}$$

Part (h): More submersion → larger $V_{sub}$ → larger $F_b$ → $T = W - F_b$ decreases.

Answers
W = 83.97 N
Part (c)
Fb = 8.72 N
Part (e)
T = 75.25 N
Part (g)

(h) Tension decreases if submerged more.

Air Speed in a Heating Duct
Problem

The main intake air duct of a forced-air heater is $0.305\text{ m}$ in diameter. The house interior is $12\text{ m} \times 19.5\text{ m} \times 2.8\text{ m}$. What is the average air speed in the duct if it cycles the entire house volume every $15\text{ min}$?

Given

$d = 0.305\text{ m}$; house: $12 \times 19.5 \times 2.8\text{ m}^3$; time: $15\text{ min} = 900\text{ s}$

Formula — Continuity

Volume flow rate must equal the house volume divided by cycle time. Then $v = Q/A$:

$$Q = \frac{V_{house}}{t} \qquad v = \frac{Q}{A_{duct}}$$
Step-by-Step
$$V_{house} = 12 \times 19.5 \times 2.8 = 655.2\text{ m}^3$$
$$Q = \frac{655.2}{900} = 0.728\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$$
$$A_{duct} = \pi\!\left(\frac{0.305}{2}\right)^{\!2} = \pi(0.1525)^2 = 0.07306\text{ m}^2$$
$$v = \frac{Q}{A} = \frac{0.728}{0.07306} = 9.96\text{ m/s}$$
Answer
v = 9.96 m/s
≈ 36 km/h inside the duct

Pitot Tube — Airspeed from Stagnation Pressure
Problem

A pitot tube measures airspeed using a mercury manometer with height $h = 0.195\text{ m}$. The density of mercury is $13.6 \times 10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$ and the density of air is $1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$. Calculate the velocity of the moving air.

Given

$h = 0.195\text{ m}$, $\rho_{Hg} = 13{,}600\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\rho_{air} = 1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$

Formula — Bernoulli's Equation

At the stagnation point all kinetic energy converts to pressure. The manometer reads the pressure difference:

$$\Delta P = \rho_{Hg}\, g\, h \qquad \Delta P = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho_{air}\, v^2$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2\,\Delta P}{\rho_{air}}}$$
Step-by-Step
$$\Delta P = (13{,}600)(9.8)(0.195) = 25{,}989.6\text{ Pa}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 25{,}989.6}{1.29}} = \sqrt{40{,}294} = 200.7\text{ m/s}$$
Answer
v = 200.7 m/s
≈ 723 km/h — mercury amplifies tiny pressure differences into readable heights

Sump Pump — Bernoulli with Elevation & Diameter Change
Problem

A sump pump operates at flow rate $Q = 0.599\text{ L/s}$ with output pressure $P_1 = 1.62 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$. The narrow pipe has diameter $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$. Part (a): Water rises to $H = 2.09\text{ m}$; find the pressure at the top (same diameter). Part (b): The pipe widens to $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$ and drops $h = 0.51\text{ m}$; find the pressure in the wider section.

Given

$Q = 0.599\text{ L/s} = 5.99 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$

$P_1 = 1.62 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$, $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$, $H = 2.09\text{ m}$, $h = 0.51\text{ m}$, $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$

Formula — Bernoulli's Equation
$$P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g z_1 = P_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g z_2$$

Speed at any cross-section from continuity: $v = Q/A$.

Step-by-Step

Find v₁ (narrow pipe):

$$A_1 = \pi(0.01315)^2 = 5.43 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^2 \qquad v_1 = \frac{Q}{A_1} = \frac{5.99\times10^{-4}}{5.43\times10^{-4}} = 1.103\text{ m/s}$$

Part (a) — same diameter, so $v_2 = v_1$; KE terms cancel:

$$P_2 = P_1 - \rho g H = 162{,}000 - (1000)(9.8)(2.09) = 162{,}000 - 20{,}482 = 141{,}518\text{ Pa}$$

Part (b) — wider pipe, continuity gives $v_3$:

$$A_3 = \pi(0.03055)^2 = 2.932\times10^{-3}\text{ m}^2 \qquad v_3 = \frac{5.99\times10^{-4}}{2.932\times10^{-3}} = 0.2043\text{ m/s}$$
$$z_3 = H - h = 2.09 - 0.51 = 1.58\text{ m}$$
$$P_3 = P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_1^2 - v_3^2) - \rho g z_3$$
= 162,000 + ½(1000)(1.103² − 0.204²) − (1000)(9.8)(1.58) = 162,000 + 500(1.217 − 0.0416) − 15,484 = 162,000 + 587.7 − 15,484 = 147,104 Pa
Answers
P₂ = 141,518 Pa
Part (a) — pressure drops as water rises
P₃ = 147,104 Pa
Part (b) — wider pipe recovers some pressure
The Physics I method: find the formula, identify knowns and unknowns, substitute, compute.
For the mathematical foundation behind each step, try A Mathematician's Way.

Force on a Circular Nail Tip
— Uniform Pressure on a Disk
Problem

Nail tips exert tremendous pressures when they are hit by a hammer because they exert a large force over a small area. What force, in newtons, must be exerted on a nail with a circular tip of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$ to create a pressure of $P = 3.17 \times 10^9\text{ Pa}$?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

You see the word pressure and immediately think: pressure is a scalar field. More precisely, $P = dF/dA$ — the instantaneous rate at which normal force accumulates per unit area. The question gives you $P$ and asks for $F$. A mathematician's instinct: integrate $P$ over the domain $A$ to recover $F$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Pressure is spatially uniform across the tip — $P(x,y) = P_0 = \text{const}$. $P$ factors out of the integral completely.

A2: Domain is a closed disk $D$ of diameter $d = 1.17\text{ mm}$, so radius $r = d/2 = 5.85\times10^{-4}\text{ m}$.

A3: Force is purely normal (perpendicular) to the area — no shear.

③ Calculus Derivation

Pressure as a surface integral, then factor out the constant:

$$F = \iint_D P(x,y)\,dA = P_0 \cdot \iint_D dA = P_0 \cdot \text{Area}(D)$$

Area of a disk in polar coordinates:

$$\text{Area}(D) = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^r \rho\,d\rho\,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\left[\frac{\rho^2}{2}\right]_0^r\!d\theta = \pi r^2$$

Plug in numerically:

r = 1.17×10⁻³ / 2 = 5.85×10⁻⁴ m A = π(5.85×10⁻⁴)² = 1.0752×10⁻⁶ m² F = 3.17×10⁹ × 1.0752×10⁻⁶ = 3408 N
④ Answer
F = 3408 N
≈ 3.41 kN — equivalent to supporting ~347 kg
⑤ Intuition

Fix total force $F$ and shrink area $A \to 0$: pressure $F/A$ blows up. This is exactly why nails work — concentrate force onto a tiny area to achieve enormous local pressure with modest total force. The integral says "add up all the little $dF = P\,dA$ contributions across the whole surface."

Hydrostatic Load on a Gas Tank
— Weight as a Surface Force
Problem

Consider a $0.505\text{ m}$-wide by $0.89\text{ m}$-long gas tank that can hold $49\text{ kg}$ of gasoline. Part (a): What pressure in N/m² is exerted on the bottom by the weight of the gasoline when full? Part (b): What is the ratio of atmospheric pressure to this pressure?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

You see "mass $m$, area $A$, find pressure." Gravity is a body force ($\rho g$ per unit volume). A mathematician asks: what is the force density acting on the bottom face? $P = F/A$, where $F$ comes from Newton: $F = mg$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Gasoline in static equilibrium — only gravity and the bottom reaction force.

A2: Bottom face is flat rectangular: $0.505\text{ m} \times 0.89\text{ m}$.

A3: Pressure from gas is uniform across the bottom (lumped mass model).

③ Calculus Derivation
$$\sum F = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; F_\text{reaction} = mg = (49)(9.8) = 480.2\text{ N}$$
$$F = \iint_B P\,dA = P \cdot (0.505)(0.89) \;\Rightarrow\; P = \frac{mg}{A}$$
P = 480.2 / 0.44945 = 1068.4 Pa Ratio: R = P_atm / P = 101325 / 1068.4 = 94.84
④ Answers
P = 1068.4 Pa
Part (a)
P₀/P = 94.84
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

Weight distributes uniformly across the bottom. Double the base area (same mass) → pressure halves. The ratio ≈95 is striking: a full tank of gasoline exerts less than 1% of atmospheric pressure. Air pushes on surfaces far harder than gasoline does.

Differential Manometry
— Integrating the Hydrostatic Gradient
Problem

A mercury manometer measures pressure inside a sealed jar of peanuts. The mercury level on the open side is $9.3\text{ cm}$ lower than on the jar side ($\rho_{Hg} = 13.5\text{ g/cm}^3$). Find the gauge pressure and the absolute pressure in the jar.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a boundary value problem for a first-order ODE. The pressure field in a static fluid satisfies a differential equation. You're given a height difference and asked for a pressure difference — literally: integrate the pressure gradient equation.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Mercury is incompressible and static: $\rho = 13{,}500\text{ kg/m}^3$ constant.

A2: Only body force is gravity, in the $-\hat{z}$ direction.

A3: Open side mercury is 9.3 cm lower than the jar side → jar is below atmospheric pressure.

③ Calculus Derivation

Euler's static equation → 1D ODE → integrate:

$$\nabla P = \rho\vec{g} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{dP}{dz} = -\rho g \quad (z\text{ upward positive})$$

Integrate from jar ($z_1$) to open side ($z_2 = z_1 - h$):

$$\int dP = \int(-\rho g)\,dz \;\Rightarrow\; P_\text{atm} - P_\text{jar} = \rho g h$$
P_g = P_jar − P_atm = −ρgh = −(13500)(9.8)(0.093) = −12,303.9 Pa P_abs = 101325 − 12304 = 89,021.1 Pa
④ Answers
Pᵍ = −12,304 Pa
Part (a)
Pₐᵦₛ = 89,021 Pa
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

$dP/dz = -\rho g$ means: going up in elevation, pressure decreases. Mercury higher on the jar side means the jar is pulling mercury up — it must be at below-atmospheric pressure. Integrating accumulates the weight of the fluid column between the two levels.

Physiological Pressure Limits
— Additive Pressure Scalars
Problem

Heroes in movies breathe through a hollow reed while hiding underwater. You cannot inhale if your lungs are more than $60.0\text{ cm}$ below the surface. What is the maximum negative gauge pressure (in cm H₂O) you can create in your lungs on dry land, given that you can achieve $-3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$ with lungs $60.0\text{ cm}$ below the surface?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a constraint satisfaction problem. Two pressure systems whose algebraic sum must equal zero for the person to inhale. Find the required value of one variable given the constraint. Think of it as: $P_\text{lungs} + P_\text{water} = 0$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: For inhalation: lung pressure must equal surface air pressure → zero net $\Delta P$ across the lung boundary.

A2: Pressure is additive: $P_\text{chest} = P_\text{water column} + P_\text{lung gauge}$. Units: cm H₂O.

A3: Max negative lung pressure relative to local outside at depth: $P_\text{lung,max} = -3.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$P_\text{ext}(z) = +z \;\text{cm H}_2\text{O (gauge)}, \quad z = \text{depth in cm}$$
$$\text{At } z = 60\text{ cm}: \quad P_\text{ext} = +60\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$$

Required lung pressure relative to surface atmosphere to inhale:

$$P_\text{required} = -(P_\text{water} + |P_\text{lung,max}|) = -(60 + 3.15) = -63.15\text{ cm H}_2\text{O}$$
④ Answer
P = −63.15 cm H₂O
Required (impossible — physiological limit is only −3.15 cm H₂O)
⑤ Intuition

Pressure is additive like scalars. On dry land, lungs fight only atmospheric pressure. Underwater, the water column adds extra force crushing the chest — the lung must overcome both simultaneously. Think of the lung as a spring trying to expand against two opposing forces at once.

Buoyancy Fraction of Ice
— Equilibrium as a Fixed-Point Equation
Problem

The fraction of ice submerged in water depends only on density. Assume $\rho_\text{ice} = 910.9\text{ kg/m}^3$ and freshwater $\rho_w = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$. What fraction of ice is submerged when it floats?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

You want a ratio: $f = V_\text{sub}/V_\text{total}$. Write the equilibrium condition as an equation in $f$, solve symbolically, then substitute numbers. No arithmetic until the algebra is done.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Static equilibrium: $\sum F = 0$ vertically.

A2: Archimedes: $F_b = \rho_w \cdot V_\text{sub} \cdot g$ (weight of displaced fluid).

A3: $\rho_\text{ice} = 910.9\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\rho_w = 1000\text{ kg/m}^3$.

③ Calculus Derivation

Archimedes follows from integrating pressure over the submerged surface (divergence theorem: $\oint P\hat{n}\,dA = \rho g V_\text{sub}$). Then:

$$F_b = W \;\Rightarrow\; \rho_w V_\text{sub} g = \rho_i V_\text{total} g$$

Cancel $g$, divide by $(\rho_w \cdot V_\text{total})$:

$$f = \frac{V_\text{sub}}{V_\text{total}} = \frac{\rho_i}{\rho_w} = \frac{910.9}{1000} = 0.9109$$
④ Answer
f = 0.9109
91.09% submerged — only ~9% visible above water
⑤ Intuition

$g$ and $V_\text{total}$ cancel completely — the fraction submerged depends only on the density ratio. Ice is 91% as dense as water, so it displaces 91% of its own volume. This is why the iceberg rule is universal regardless of size.

6
Static Equilibrium of a Suspended Cube
— Three-Force Balance
Problem

A cube with edge length $L = 0.13\text{ m}$ and density $\rho_c = 3.9\times10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$ is exactly half submerged in a liquid of density $\rho_l = 0.81\times10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$, suspended by a taut string. Find: (c) weight $W$ in N, (e) buoyant force $F_b$ in N, (g) tension $T$ in N, (h) what happens to $T$ if submerged more?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

$\sum F = 0$ is a linear equation in $\{W, F_b, T\}$. Parameterize each force symbolically in terms of $(\rho_c, \rho_l, L, g)$, reduce to one scalar equation, solve. Since all forces are collinear (vertical), it's a one-dimensional problem.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Static equilibrium → $T + F_b = W$ (upward positive).

A2: Exactly half submerged: $V_\text{sub} = L^3/2$.

A3: $L = 0.13\text{ m}$, $\rho_c = 3900\text{ kg/m}^3$, $\rho_l = 810\text{ kg/m}^3$, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$W = \iiint_\text{cube} \rho_c g\,dV = \rho_c g L^3$$
$$F_b = \rho_l g \frac{L^3}{2} \qquad \sum F = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; T = W - F_b$$
W = (3900)(9.8)(0.13)³ = (3900)(9.8)(0.002197) = 83.97 N F_b = (810)(9.8)(0.002197/2) = (810)(9.8)(0.001099) = 8.72 N T = 83.97 − 8.72 = 75.25 N Part (h): more submersion → V_sub↑ → F_b↑ → T↓ (decreases)
④ Answers
W = 83.97 N
Part (c)
Fb = 8.72 N
Part (e)
T = 75.25 N
Part (g)
⑤ Intuition

Tension is what's "left over" after buoyancy partially supports the cube. If $\rho_c = \rho_l$, the cube floats freely and $T = 0$. Deeper submersion → more displaced fluid → more buoyancy → string relaxes.

7
Arterial Blood Flow
— Flux Integral over a Circular Cross-Section
Problem

Blood flows through an artery of radius $r = 2.3\text{ mm}$ at speed $v = 42\text{ cm/s}$. Part (a): Find the flow rate $Q$ (cm³/s). Part (b): Find the volume $V$ passing through in $30.0\text{ s}$.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Flow rate $Q$ is a flux integral: $Q = \iint_A \vec{v} \cdot \hat{n}\,dA$. What velocity field is assumed, and what is the domain $A$? Once $Q$ is known, volume $= \int Q\,dt$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Plug flow — $v$ is uniform across the cross-section.

A2: Cross-section is a disk of radius $r = 0.23\text{ cm}$.

A3: $Q$ is constant over 30 s, so $V = Q \cdot t$ (no further integration).

③ Calculus Derivation
$$Q = \iint_D \vec{v}\cdot\hat{n}\,dA = v\cdot\iint_D dA = v \cdot \pi r^2$$
$$V = \int_0^{30} Q\,dt = Q \cdot 30$$
Q = 42 × π(0.23)² = 42 × 0.16619 = 6.980 cm³/s V = 6.980 × 30 = 209.4 cm³
④ Answers
Q = 6.98 cm³/s
Part (a)
V = 209.4 cm³
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

$Q = A \cdot v$: in one second, all the blood in a cylinder of length $v$ and cross-section $A$ passes through. Volume = how many such cylinders pass in time $t$? Exactly $Q \cdot t$ of them.

8
Air Duct Velocity
— Continuity / Conservation of Volume
Problem

A forced-air heater has a main duct of diameter $0.305\text{ m}$. The house interior is $12\text{ m} \times 19.5\text{ m} \times 2.8\text{ m}$. What is the average air speed in the duct if it cycles the entire house volume every $15\text{ min}$?

① How a Mathematician Reads This

This is a conservation law: total volume flux is conserved. You know the total volume that must move per unit time (room volume ÷ cycle time) and the pipe cross-section. Solve for the one unknown velocity: $Q_\text{room} = Q_\text{duct}$.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Air is incompressible (low speed): $\nabla \cdot \vec{v} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; Q_\text{in} = Q_\text{out}$.

A2: House volume $12 \times 19.5 \times 2.8\text{ m}^3$ exchanged every $15\text{ min} = 900\text{ s}$.

A3: Duct cross-section: disk of diameter $0.305\text{ m}$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$\oint_S \vec{v}\cdot\hat{n}\,dA = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; Q_\text{in} = Q_\text{out} = Q$$
Q = V_room / t = (12 × 19.5 × 2.8) / 900 = 655.2 / 900 = 0.7280 m³/s A_duct = π(0.305/2)² = π(0.1525)² = 0.07306 m² v = Q / A = 0.7280 / 0.07306 = 9.96 m/s
④ Answer
v = 9.96 m/s
≈ 36 km/h inside the duct
⑤ Intuition

Incompressible → can't "store" mass inside. Room sets the demand $Q$; duct area sets the geometry; velocity adjusts to satisfy both. Narrow duct → same $Q$ → higher speed.

9
Pitot Tube Stagnation Pressure
— Bernoulli as a First Integral
Problem

A pitot tube measures airspeed using a mercury manometer with height $h = 0.195\text{ m}$. Given $\rho_{Hg} = 13.6\times10^3\text{ kg/m}^3$ and $\rho_{air} = 1.29\text{ kg/m}^3$, calculate the velocity of the moving air.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Bernoulli's equation is the first integral of Euler's equation along a streamline. Integrate the momentum ODE to get a conserved quantity — total mechanical energy per unit volume. Apply as a two-point boundary condition to find velocity.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Steady, inviscid, incompressible flow; freestream speed $v$ → stagnation $v_2 = 0$.

A2: No elevation change: $z_1 = z_2$, so $\rho g z$ terms cancel.

A3: Manometer reads $\Delta P = \rho_{Hg} \cdot g \cdot h$, $h = 0.195\text{ m}$.

③ Calculus Derivation

Euler along streamline (arc length $s$):

$$\rho v\frac{dv}{ds} = -\frac{dP}{ds} - \rho g\frac{dz}{ds}$$

Rearranging → first integral (conserved along streamline):

$$\frac{d}{ds}\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + P + \rho gz\right] = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + P + \rho gz = \text{const}$$

Apply point 1 → point 2 ($v_2 = 0$, same height):

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\rho_\text{air} v^2 + P_1 = P_2 \;\Rightarrow\; \Delta P = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho_\text{air} v^2 \;\Rightarrow\; v = \sqrt{\frac{2\Delta P}{\rho_\text{air}}}$$
ΔP = ρ_Hg · g · h = (13600)(9.8)(0.195) = 25,989.6 Pa v = √(2 × 25989.6 / 1.29) = √(40294) = 200.7 m/s
④ Answer
v = 200.7 m/s
≈ 723 km/h
⑤ Intuition

Slowing fluid to zero at stagnation converts ALL kinetic energy to pressure. Mercury is ~10,000× denser than air — even a tiny $\Delta P$ produces a measurable column height, amplifying small pressure signals into readable measurements.

10
Sump Pump Energy Conservation
— Full Bernoulli with Elevation + Velocity
Problem

A sump pump operates at $Q = 0.599\text{ L/s}$ with output pressure $P_1 = 1.62\times10^5\text{ Pa}$ through a pipe of diameter $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$. Part (a): Water rises to height $H = 2.09\text{ m}$ in the same-diameter pipe; find the pressure at the top. Part (b): The pipe widens to $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$ and drops $h = 0.51\text{ m}$; find the pressure in the wider section.

① How a Mathematician Reads This

Bernoulli with all three terms varying simultaneously: pressure, velocity (pipe diameter changes → continuity forces velocity change), and elevation. Set the conserved quantity at point 1 (known) equal to points 2 and 3 (unknowns). Two equations, two unknowns.

② Starting Assumptions

A1: Steady, inviscid, incompressible — Bernoulli holds.

A2: Point 1: $P_1 = 1.62\times10^5\text{ Pa}$, $d = 2.63\text{ cm}$, $Q = 5.99\times10^{-4}\text{ m}^3/\text{s}$, $z_1 = 0$.

A3 (Part a): Same diameter to point 2 → $v_2 = v_1$, $z_2 = H = 2.09\text{ m}$.

A4 (Part b): $D = 6.11\text{ cm}$ at point 3, $z_3 = H - h = 2.09 - 0.51 = 1.58\text{ m}$.

③ Calculus Derivation
$$P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gz = \text{const} \quad \text{(Bernoulli conserved quantity)}$$
v₁ = Q/A₁ = 5.99×10⁻⁴ / π(0.01315)² = 1.103 m/s ── Part (a): same diameter → v₂ = v₁ → KE terms cancel ── P₂ = P₁ − ρgH = 162000 − (1000)(9.8)(2.09) = 141,518 Pa ── Part (b): wider pipe, continuity gives v₃ ── A₃ = π(0.03055)² = 2.932×10⁻³ m² v₃ = Q/A₃ = 5.99×10⁻⁴ / 2.932×10⁻³ = 0.2043 m/s P₃ = P₁ + ½ρ(v₁² − v₃²) − ρg·z₃ = 162000 + 500(1.2166 − 0.04174) − (1000)(9.8)(1.58) = 162000 + 587.4 − 15484 = 147,103 Pa
④ Answers
P₂ = 141,518 Pa
Part (a)
P₃ = 147,103 Pa
Part (b)
⑤ Intuition

Bernoulli = work-energy theorem integrated: pressure + kinetic + potential = const. Going uphill (Part a): potential rises → pressure drops. Wider pipe (Part b): fluid slows (continuity) → kinetic energy drops → converts back to pressure, partially compensating the elevation loss. This is the Venturi effect in reverse.

All derivations reduce to two ideas:
$F = \displaystyle\iint P\,dA$  (pressure as surface integral)  &  $\dfrac{d}{ds}\!\left[P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gz\right] = 0$  (Bernoulli as first integral of momentum).
Everything else is calculus.
